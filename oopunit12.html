Blog Post: Emerging Trends in Information Systems

The development of Bitcoin introduced Blockchain technology to the world (previously only existing in a research context) (Strawn 2019).

A blockchain is made up of blocks. Each block contains (Williams 2019):

  • Some data (this depends on the blockchain, for example bitcoin contains: sender, receiver, and number of points/amount of bitcoin).
  • The hash of the block (like a fingerprint, it is unique and identifies a block). Changing something inside the block will cause the hash to change. If it changes then it no longer is the same block. The hash ensures the content of the block has not been changed, but does not reveal the specifics inside the block.
  • The hash of the previous block (this creates a chain of blocks and what makes blockchain so secure). If someone tampers with, for example, the second block then the hash of the block changes then this causes all subsequent blocks to become invalid.

Williams (2019) and Strawn (2019) write that there are further security mechanisms within blockchain. One of these is proof-of-work. This mechanism slows down the creation of new blocks. This makes it too time-consuming to tamper with a block, as you have to recalculate all other blocks in the chain.

Williams (2019) and Strawn (2019) write that peer-to-peer networks add to the above security mechanisms. A digital ledger is shared between all participants in the chain and cannot be amended or deleted. Blockchain is not managed by any centralised entity. Anyone is allowed to join, and when they do, they get a full copy of the blockchain. All nodes of the network need consensus, so if anyone tampers with a block they would need to control the other users on the peer-to-peer network to be successful. Consensus is needed to create new blocks.

Williams (2019) makes the argument that blockchain will impact every section of technology. Including, but not limited to, IOT devices, logistics, healthcare, networking and finance.

Taking the example of healthcare, Tanwar et al (2020) state that blockchain could be used for decentralised storage of and retrieval of patient healthcare records. Providing a means of verifying and ensuring the integrity of patient data. All healthcare providers could be part of the blockchain architecture and patients are in possession of a private key. Then, when a healthcare provider has to access blockchain they will access the patient’s public ID (containing no personal information) and the patient provides the private key (blockchain intrinsically contains a high level of encryption and from just having the hash the content of the block is not readable).

One of the biggest downsides to blockchain technology is energy consumption. Corbet et al (2010) write that Bitcoin uses the energy consumption of a small developed country due to the processing power that is involved. This has major cost and environmental implications for using Blockchain technology in other areas of life. However, Bitcoin is necessary data-intensive, as the creation of new Bitcoin, through mining, needs to be processor-heavy, as if it could be created with ease then it would lack the main feature of physical currency (i.e. it cannot be created easily) (Williams 2019). Blockchain uses a cryptographic, computationally intensive puzzle to create new Bitcoin. Sedlmeir et al (2020) write that other areas of blockchain do not need the features contained in Bitcoin and therefore would not have to be so energy-intensive.
